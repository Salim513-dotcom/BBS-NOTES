üìèConcept of Measurement

What is Measurement?

Assigning symbols or numbers to any product or event or issue or characteristics using a specified rule is called measurement.

Note: Measuring physical quantities is very easy in practice but qualitative types of any issues' measurement is not so easy.

Physical vs Qualitative Measurement:

Easy to measure: Length, height, mass, etc.

Difficult to measure: Sadness, satisfaction, happiness, etc.

Example - Measuring Satisfaction:

Satisfaction Level: Highly Satisfied Satisfied Less Satisfied

"Measurement is a rule of tools which provides a series of numbers to the series of the characteristic of events, products or any things."

I. Validity

Validity refers to the extent you are measuring what you to measure which indicates the accuracy. A valid measurement reflects only the characteristics of interest and random error.

Definitions:

Seltiz: "Validity of measuring instrument is defined as the property of a measure that allows the researcher to say that the instrument measures what he says measures."

Hatt & Goode: "A scale possesses its validity when it actually measures what it claims to measures."

Classification of Validity:

Content Validity

Ensures measuring tools include adequate and representative set of items. Also called face validity.

Construct Validity

Agreement between theoretical concept and specific measuring device. Two types: Convergent and Discriminant.

Criteria Related Validity

Helps predict certain possibility. Must be reliable, relevant, free from bias, and available.

II. Reliability

A measurement device is reliable when it will produce consistent result about the same result when applied to the same sample or to different samples of the same size taken from the same population.

Qualities of Reliability:

  • Stability: Stable and consistent result with same instrument on same sample
  • Equivalency: Different tests give equivalent results
  • Internal Consistency: Similar instruments produce highly correlated responses

Measurements of Reliability:

a. Test-Retest Method: Use same instrument multiple times on same sample and compare correlation.

b. Alternative/Parallel Forms: Develop two or more forms for same sample and compare results.

c. Split Half Method: Divide instrument into two halves (odd/even or randomly) and compare correlation.

III. Practicability

Instrument used to measure the attitude of people must be applicable in real practice.

Qualities:

  • Economic: Select design within budget, reduce expenditures
  • Convenience: Select nearby field, not difficult places
  • Interpretable: Easy to demonstrate and describe data

üìêScales Construction for Attitude Measurement

I. Arbitrary Scale

If a researcher thinks about the completely new matter and uses his own scale to measure the attitude of people then this type of scale is called arbitrary scale.

Important: Researcher must be able to prove the validity and reliability of the scale and the measurement used. Normally used in social science.

II. Summated Rating Scale / Likert Scale

This scale was developed by Rensis Likert in 1962. According to him, degree of the agreement and disagreement with the given series of the statement is measured by making a series of the alternative.

Example:

Question: Are you satisfied with the structural organization?

1 2 3 4 5
Fully Disagree Disagree Undecided Agree Fully Agree

Note: Likert Scales can have 5, 7, or 9 options depending on condition.

Construction Procedures:

  1. Collect many statements relevant to the attitude
  2. Collect questionnaires to measure similar concept
  3. Change statements to proper questions
  4. Collect responses in options of 5, 7 or 9
  5. Add scores individually and calculate sum
  6. Include only statements that correlate with total test
  7. Calculate resultant scores and make final evaluation

III. Differential Rating Scale / Thurstone Scale

This approach was developed by L.L. Thurstone. This method selects the items made by panel of judges who evaluate the items and they are relevant to the topic.

Procedures:

  1. Make many descriptions related to the concept
  2. Present statements to panel of judges in ascending/descending order
  3. Remove disagreed items from panel
  4. Calculate mean from respondents' answers
  5. Remove long, unclear and non-related descriptions
  6. Arrange agree/disagree series with all possibilities
  7. Fix number for each view and calculate

Example - Employees' Attitude:

Statement Score
I am satisfied with this organization 8.5
If I leave this organization, I face problem 7.5
Working environment is good 4.6
Rules and regulations are clear 6.2

Example Calculation: If statements 1 and 3 are ticked: Average = (8.5 + 4.6)/2 = 6.5

IV. Cumulative Rating Scale / Guttman Scale

This scale was developed by Louis Guttman. A scalogram is made with the help of the statement derived and agree or disagree is cumulated.

Assumption: If one replies favorable to statement #5, then he/she is in favor of #1, 2, 3, and 4. If unfavorable on #3, then also unfavorable on #1 and #2.

Construction Steps:

  1. Selection of clear statements to be presented
  2. Pre-test on sample of around 100 people
  3. More than 80 people must give opinion
  4. Arrange statements based on high/low response
  5. Remove unclear responses
  6. Calculate coefficient of reproducibility: (1 - No of errors) / No of responses (must be 0.90 or higher)
  7. Assess value based on favorable answers

üìäFactors Affecting Sample Size

1. Homogeneity/Heterogeneity of Universe

If comparative similarity exists among groups, sample may represent homogeneous character. If nature varies, sample should be large.

2. Nature of Classes Proposed

If we need to form large number of classes in population, sample size will be large. Less classes means smaller sample size.

3. Nature of Study

In-depth study needs small sample. More time and expenses required. Nature of sample must be made easy to handle.

4. Practical Consideration

Time and resources determine sample size. More samples need more time and expenses. Must make it practical.

5. Standard of Accuracy

A small but properly selected sample can give better result. If required accuracy is high, large sample size is better.

6. Nature of Cases to be Contacted

If cases are geographically dispersed and chances of refusal are high, sample size should be large.

7. Type of Sampling Used

Absolute random sampling requires large sample. Stratified sampling can achieve reliability with smaller sample if stratification is proper.

üîÑSampling Process

1

Define the Population

Define people, age, gender, income, education, behavior etc. under certain demographically specified area.

2

Specify the Sampling Frame

Frame is a list of elements from which sample is drawn. Get full, accurate and updated list of all units.

3

Specify Sample Unit

Choose a sampling unit that represents every character of population. May be geographical unit like state, district, municipality, village or ward.

4

Determine Sample Size

Number of items to be selected. Should be optimal - not excessively large nor too small. Must fulfill requirements of efficiency, representativeness, reliability.

5

Prepare Plan for Sampling

Formulate plan to make sampling well managed. Sampling plan provides guidelines to operationalize the sampling design and size.

6

Select the Sample

Selected sample should represent population and be useful to attain goal of research. Must be properly representative for generalization.

üé≤I. Probability Sampling

a. Simple Random Sampling

A sampling method in which every element has equal chance to be selected as a sample. Selection may be dependent on incident and no one can estimate which element will be selected.

Methods: Lottery, random number set, computerized lottery, etc.

b. Systematic Sampling

One item is selected at random and then Kth interval is obtained to find next items.

Formula: K = Size of population (N) / Size of sample (n)

Example: Bank has 600 employees. Want 10% sample.

K = 600/10 = 60 samples needed

If random number is 6, then: 6, 66, 126, 186... (60 participants)

c. Stratified Sampling

Take a combination of different groups of population having different nature and representatives are taken from each group under prescribed ratio or proportion.

Strata: Overlapping homogeneous groups present in population (by age, sex, qualification, employment, etc.)

Proportionate: Selection in same proportion

Disproportionate: Selection with less or more in sub-groups

Example - Babil Bank Managers Meet:

Level Total Proportionate Disproportionate
High 20 2 5
Middle 30 3 2
Low 50 5 3
Total 100 10 10

d. Cluster Sampling

Cluster is a heterogeneous group in the population and can be considered as small population. A sampling is called cluster sampling if a cluster is randomly selected having all elements of the population.

Use Case: Normally done for large population when difficult to reach each unit or element. Makes easy and convenient to collect data from wide range.

üéØII. Non-Probability Sampling

Characteristics:

  • Researcher makes proper pre-plan to choose sample
  • No equal chance of selecting sample
  • Low cost method
  • Consumes less time
  • Needs less effort
  • May be full of bias
  • Normally cannot be generalized to total population

a. Purposive/Judgmental Sampling

Researcher selects the unit or element or sample himself/herself to fulfill his/her objective. Economic and used by expert researchers.

Example: Effectiveness of bank training is to be evaluated. Researcher takes data from participants who were present in training by asking them to fill form or seeing them in field whether they use learnt matter.

b. Quota Sampling

Heterogeneous samples present in population taken as groups based on different nature, features and qualities, and one is taken from each category fixing quota.

Example - Students Using Different SIMs:

Service Provider 12-16yrs 16-18yrs 18-20yrs Total
NTC 50 (5) 150 (4) 100 (3) 300 (12)
Ncell 40 (7) 60 (2) 100 (6) 200 (15)
Smart 50 (7) 30 (2) 20 (3) 100 (12)
Total 140 (19) 240 (8) 220 (12) 600 (39)

Quota fixed at 39 persons selected from 600 students

c. Convenience/Accidental Sampling

Researcher selects units as sample on basis of convenience. Done for easiness taking samples nearby. Not much reliable, normally done for pre-testing questionnaire. Low cost and less time consuming.

d. Self-Selecting Sampling

Researcher gives information through media to respondent and respondents provide information to the media.

e. Snow Ball/Reference Sampling

Researcher starts from one sample and taking reference from him/her, next is chosen. The next one gives reference to next again in a continuous chain. Number of respondents increases according to necessity.

Example: Police investigation in a murder done by unknown person in a gang fight.

‚ö†Ô∏èSampling Error

What is Sampling Error?

A sampling error is an error which is made in selecting samples that are not representative of the population. It is the difference between the sample value and true value of the population mean.

Common Sampling Errors:

  • 1. Population Specification Error: Due to less understanding of who should be surveyed (e.g., Father instead of child)
  • 2. Sample Frame Error: Using wrong frame (e.g., phone list for those who don't use phones)
  • 3. Selection Error: Selected sample is not available
  • 4. Non Response: Respondent does not speak
  • 5. Error in Taking Sample: Too large or too small sample size and population
